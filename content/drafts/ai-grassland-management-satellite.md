# Can Satellite Data and AI Actually Help You Manage Grass Better? What's Available in Ireland

Grass is Ireland's biggest competitive advantage in farming. Teagasc has been saying it for years: every extra tonne of grass utilised per hectare is worth roughly €173 on a dairy farm and €105 on a beef farm. The numbers are clear.

What's less clear is whether the new wave of satellite-based tools and AI platforms can actually help you grow and use more of it — or whether they're tech solutions looking for a problem that a plate meter and a notebook already solve.

We looked at what's available, what it costs, and what Irish farmers are actually finding when they use it.

---

## What satellite grass measurement actually means

The basic idea is simple. Satellites — mostly the EU's Sentinel-2 — pass over Ireland every five days and capture images in wavelengths that tell you how much biomass is on the ground. Software processes those images and gives you a grass cover estimate for each paddock.

In theory, you get a grass wedge without walking the farm.

In practice, there are important caveats.

---

## What's actually available to Irish farmers

### PastureBase Ireland (free — Teagasc)

PastureBase is Teagasc's grassland decision support tool. It doesn't use satellite data directly for cover measurement — it relies on farmer-entered measurements (plate meter or visual). But it's the foundation that everything else plugs into.

- **Cost:** Free
- **What it does:** Stores measurements, generates grass wedges, benchmarks you against other farms
- **Limitation:** You still have to walk the farm and enter data manually
- **Verdict:** Essential. If you're not on PastureBase, start here before looking at anything else.

### Satellite-based platforms (various commercial)

Several commercial platforms now offer satellite-derived grass cover estimates for Irish farms:

- **How they work:** You draw your paddock boundaries on a map. The platform pulls Sentinel-2 imagery and converts the spectral data into estimated grass covers (kg DM/ha).
- **Update frequency:** Every 5 days when cloud-free. This is the key issue in Ireland.
- **Cost:** Ranges from free tiers to €5–15/ha/year for premium features
- **Accuracy:** Studies show satellite estimates correlate reasonably well with plate meter readings in ideal conditions — but accuracy drops in cloudy weather, after slurry spreading, and on fields with uneven sward composition.

### AI-enhanced platforms

Some newer platforms layer AI on top of satellite data to:

- Predict grass growth rates 7–14 days ahead
- Suggest optimal grazing rotation sequences
- Flag paddocks that are falling behind target covers
- Integrate weather forecasts with growth models

These are genuinely useful when they work. The prediction models are trained on Irish data (from PastureBase and Met Éireann), which gives them a real advantage over generic global tools.

---

## The Ireland problem: cloud cover

This is the elephant in the room. Sentinel-2 captures optical imagery — it can't see through clouds. Ireland has cloud cover on roughly 70% of days.

What this means in practice:

- In a wet April, you might get one usable satellite image in three weeks
- Exactly when you most need grass data (spring rotation planning), the satellite is least reliable
- Platforms handle this differently — some interpolate between readings, some show gaps

Radar-based satellites (Sentinel-1) can see through clouds, and some platforms are starting to incorporate radar data. But radar grass measurement is less mature and less accurate than optical.

---

## What farmers are actually saying

We spoke to farmers and advisors using these tools. The consensus:

**What works:**
- Summer monitoring is genuinely useful — satellite updates are consistent May to September
- The overview effect is valuable — seeing all paddocks on one screen catches things you miss walking
- Growth prediction models are improving year on year
- It saves time on large farms (50+ paddocks) where walking every paddock weekly is a real time cost

**What doesn't:**
- Spring reliability is poor when you need it most
- Satellite covers don't match plate meter readings exactly — there's always a margin of error
- Small paddocks (under 1 hectare) don't resolve well in satellite imagery
- You still need to ground-truth with a plate meter at key decision points

---

## The honest verdict: mixed

Satellite grass tools are not a replacement for walking your farm with a plate meter. Not yet. The cloud cover problem in Ireland is real and it's the one thing the technology can't fix quickly.

But as a **supplement** — especially for larger farms, for summer management, and for the overview that helps you spot problems early — they're worth looking at. The free or low-cost options let you try before you commit.

**Our recommendation:**

1. Get on PastureBase first. It's free and it's the foundation.
2. Try a satellite platform's free tier over one grazing season. Don't pay for a full year upfront.
3. Keep your plate meter. Use satellite data to prioritise which paddocks to measure, not to replace measuring.
4. Watch the AI prediction features — they're the most promising part and they're improving fast.

---

## The bottom line

The technology is real. The Ireland-specific limitations are also real. Right now, satellite grass tools are a useful extra set of eyes — not a replacement for the ones you've got. Give it two to three more years of development, especially on the cloud cover workarounds, and this space will look very different.

For now: try it for free, use it where it works, and don't throw away the plate meter.
