# FarmAI Ireland — Content Strategy

*March 2026 · Update quarterly*

---

## Our Lane in One Sentence

We don't cover farming news. We cover how AI fits into your farming life.

Teagasc tells farmers what to do. The Farmers Journal tells them what's happening. FarmAI Ireland tells them how to use the tools that are changing everything — in plain English, for free.

---

## 0. The Core Filter

> Everything on this site exists to save Irish farmers time, money, or headache. If it doesn't do one of those three things, it doesn't belong here.

Every article title must explicitly state what the farmer saves. Not "How to use X" — but "Save Y by using X" or "Stop wasting Z with X." The reason to read comes before the method.

We are ambassadors and enablers. We don't own the farm. We can't test tools in the field ourselves. But we can read everything, filter the noise, and translate what matters — so the farmer doesn't have to.

---

## 1. Editorial Position

FarmAI Ireland exists in a gap that nobody else is filling. Mainstream agri media covers news, prices, and policy. Teagasc covers best practice. Nobody is sitting beside the farmer and saying: here's this AI thing you've been hearing about — here's what it actually does, here's whether it's worth your time, and here's exactly how to use it on your farm.

That's our job. We are ambassadors and enablers — translators, not authorities.

### The Three Editorial Lanes

Every piece of content we publish fits into one of three lanes. If it doesn't fit any lane, we don't publish it.

| Lane | The question it answers | Example |
|------|------------------------|---------|
| Lane 1: Can AI help me with this? | Practical how-to guides | "How to use your phone to track cattle movements for ICBF" |
| Lane 2: What's changing that I need to know? | Policy and tech translated into farm impact | "What the new EU AI Act means for agri software you're already using" |
| Lane 3: Is this worth my time and money? | Honest cost-benefit, Irish context | "We tested three AI farm management apps. Here's the honest verdict." |

### What We Are Not

- We are not a news site. We don't cover mart prices, weather, or breaking policy.
- We are not Teagasc. We don't give professional advice. We signpost to it.
- We are not a product review site paid by vendors. Every opinion is independent.
- We are not for tech enthusiasts. We write for farmers who've never used AI and are suspicious of it.

---

## 2. Audience

Our primary reader is a working Irish farmer — dairy, beef, sheep, or tillage — aged 35–60, phone in hand more than they'd like to admit, time-poor, and deeply sceptical of anything that sounds like a sales pitch. They've heard AI mentioned at the mart, on the radio, and in the Journal. They don't know what it means for their farm specifically. They're not going to spend three hours researching it. They will spend five minutes reading something that gets to the point.

### Persona: The Reader We're Writing For

**Eoin, 47, Roscommon beef farmer**

Runs 80 suckler cows, does his own paperwork, uses his phone for calls and weather.

He's heard AI mentioned twice this month — once at the mart, once on Shannonside. He's mildly curious but mostly suspicious.

He'll read something if it's short, honest, and tells him what to actually do. He'll close it the second it feels like a sales pitch or talks down to him.

He doesn't call it a blog. He calls it "a piece he read online."

Secondary audiences: agricultural students, Teagasc advisors staying current, agri journalists, rural councillors, and agri-business commercial teams.

## 3. Content Types

### Type A: How-To Guide (core product)

- Length: 600–900 words
- Format: Problem → Tool → Step-by-step → What it costs → Where to get help
- Tone: Neighbour over the gate. Not professor at a podium.
- Frequency: 2 per week
- Must include: cited source, OfficialAdviceBanner if grants/health, sources footnote
- Example: "How to use ChatGPT to write a BISS application (and what to check before you submit)"

### Type B: Weekly Roundup (low effort, high consistency)

- Length: 300–400 words
- Format: 3–5 things that happened in AI/agri this week, translated into farm impact
- Source: Teagasc news, gov.ie scheme updates, agri Twitter, EU CAP portal
- Tone: Dry, honest, one farmer talking to another
- Frequency: Every Monday, generated via Claude weekly roundup prompt
- Example: "This week in farm AI: what the Teagasc grass app update means for you"

### Type C: Tool Review (builds trust, drives repeat visits)

- Length: 800–1,200 words
- Format: What it is → What it costs → Tried it on a real farm task → Verdict
- Frequency: 2–3 per month once established
- Example: "We used AI to plan a five-field rotation. Here's what it got right and where it fell flat."

### Type D: Explainer (education, SEO, long tail)

- Length: 400–600 words
- Format: Plain-English answer to one specific farmer question
- Sourced from: Ask FarmAI contact form submissions, social comments
- Example: "What exactly is a large language model and should a farmer care?"

## 6. Quality Filter — The Anti-Slop Check

Before every article is published, run it through this checklist. If it fails more than two, it goes back for revision.

| Check | Pass criteria |
|-------|---------------|
| The so-what test | First 100 words answer: what does this mean for my farm? |
| The Eoin test | Would a 47-year-old Roscommon beef farmer read past paragraph 2? |
| The jargon test | Every technical term is explained in plain English on first use |
| The source test | At least one real source cited (Teagasc, gov.ie, ICBF, etc.) |
| The advice test | OfficialAdviceBanner present on any grants/livestock health content |
| The slop test | No AI-sounding phrases: "in conclusion", "it's worth noting", "in today's rapidly evolving landscape" |
| The value test | Title and first 100 words explicitly state what the reader saves (time, money, or headache) |
| The length test | No padding. If it's under 500 words and covers the topic, that's fine. |
