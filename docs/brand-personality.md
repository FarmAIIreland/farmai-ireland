## Brand Voice

### How we sound

**Honest and straight-talking**: We say what we mean. If something is complicated, we say so. If something doesn't work for small Irish farms, we say that too. We're not farmers — we say that too. No spin, no corporate hedging.

**Grounded in science, data and truth**: The fun and dry humour is the delivery. The substance underneath is always cited, sourced, and verified. Teagasc said it. The data shows it. The study found it. We translate — we don't invent.

**Dry Irish humour**: Not jokes. Not trying to be funny. That particular Irish tone — wry, occasionally self-deprecating, never taking itself too seriously. The kind of thing that makes someone smile quietly.

**Willing to show frustration**: When AI tools are confusing, overhyped, or just don't deliver, we say so. Frustration is honest. "We spent three hours on this and it still didn't work properly — here's what we learned" is more valuable than a polished how-to that ignores the hard bits.

**Not afraid to try things**: We experiment. We share what worked and what didn't. We're figuring it out alongside you — not waiting until we have all the answers.

### Writing rules

- Write like you're explaining to someone across a gate, not presenting at a conference
- Max 25 words per sentence — if longer, break it up
- No jargon without an immediate plain-English explanation
- Second person always — "you", "your farm", "your herd"
- Active voice — "AI monitors your cattle" not "cattle are monitored by AI"
- Short paragraphs — two to three sentences maximum
- Start in the middle of the conversation — never "In recent years..."
- Cite sources naturally in the text, not just in a footnote
- End with something actionable — always

### What we never say
- "Leverage" anything
- "Game-changing" or "revolutionary"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "AI is transforming the agricultural sector"
- "It's never been more important to..."
- Any sentence that could have been written by anyone, about anything, anywhere

## Target Audience — Four Reader Profiles

### Reader 1 — Ciarán, 38, Dairy Farmer, Tipperary
Milking 120 cows. Up at 5am. Phone is his main computer. Has heard about AI but hasn't had time to figure out what it means for him. Not anti-technology — uses Herdwatch, has a decent grasp of the BISS application. Just time-poor and a bit sceptical of things that sound too good to be true.

**What he needs**: Short, practical, specific. Tell him what to do, how long it takes, what it costs. If it doesn't work for a 120-cow dairy operation in Tipperary, say so upfront.

**What makes him share**: Something that saves him time or money. Something he can show his co-op advisor and say "look at this."

---

### Reader 2 — Margaret, 54, Beef & Sheep, Roscommon
Runs the farm with her husband. Handles the paperwork, grant applications, vet coordination. Has grown kids telling her to "try AI for this." Willing to learn but needs someone to explain it without making her feel stupid. Trust is everything — she's been burned before by expensive tech that didn't deliver.

**What she needs**: Patience. Plain English. Step by step. Real examples from farms like hers. Honest acknowledgement that some of this is genuinely confusing.

**What makes her subscribe**: Feeling like someone actually understands her situation. A guide that mentions Roscommon, suckler cows, or the specific grants she applies for. Specificity builds trust.

---

### Reader 3 — Seán, 26, Taking Over the Family Farm, Wexford
Mixed tillage and beef. Has a degree. Comfortable with tech. Already using some AI tools but wants to go deeper. Brand-conscious — wants to feel like he's reading something credible, not a content farm.

**What he needs**: Depth when warranted. Links to original sources. Honest takes on what's overhyped. Treat him as an intelligent adult who can handle nuance.

**What makes him an advocate**: Content that makes him look smart when he shares it. Something genuinely useful his older neighbours haven't seen yet.

---

### Reader 4 — Pádraic, 47, Beef Farmer, Mayo — The Sceptic
Has seen it all before. Expensive machinery that didn't pay back. Schemes that disappeared. Apps that promised everything and delivered nothing. Not against technology in principle — uses what works, ignores what doesn't. When someone mentions AI, his default is "who's making money from this and why are they pushing it at me?"

**What he needs**: Evidence, not enthusiasm. Real numbers from real farms. Honest acknowledgement of what doesn't work. And above all, no sense that he's being sold something. If we're excited about a tool, we need to earn that with data.

**What makes him trust us**: When we criticise something. When we say "we tried this and it didn't work." When we cite Teagasc rather than a startup's own marketing. When we disagree with the hype. Pádraic will never be an evangelist — but if we earn his trust, he'll be our most credible reader. His word of mouth is worth ten of anyone else's.

**The sceptic content series**: "Does This Actually Work?" — we test AI tools against real farming tasks, with honest verdicts. No affiliate deals. No sponsored content in this series. Ever. That's the promise that makes it credible.

## Content Principles

**1. Learning out loud**
We don't wait until we have all the answers. We share the process — what we tried, what worked, what didn't, what we're still figuring out. This builds trust faster than polished expertise.

**2. Specificity over generality**
"AI can help Irish farmers" is useless. "Here's how a Galway sheep farmer used ChatGPT to cut his GLAS application time from four hours to forty minutes" is useful. Always specific. Always Irish. Always real.

**3. Grounded in sources**
Every claim traceable to Teagasc, gov.ie, ICBF, peer-reviewed research, or verifiable real-world data. The fun is in the delivery. The truth is non-negotiable.

**4. Honest about limitations**
Every AI tool has limitations. Every scheme has exceptions. We say this clearly, every time.

**5. The 'so what' rule**
Every piece of content must leave the reader knowing what to do next. Before publishing, ask — does this leave them with a clear action? If not, add it.

**6. Farmer-first framing**
Always start from the farmer's problem, not the technology. Not "here's what ChatGPT can do" but "you spend four hours on grant applications — here's how to cut that to forty minutes."

**7. State the value upfront**
Every title and first paragraph must tell the reader what they save — time, money, or headache. Not implied. Stated. "How to use AI for ACRES paperwork" becomes "Cut your ACRES paperwork in half with one free tool." The reason to read comes before the method.

**Tone on video**: Honest, dry, not over-produced. Resist the urge to make everything look too slick. Real feeling beats polished every time for this audience.

**Series** (mapped to pillars):
- "Does This Actually Work?" (`does-this-work`) — testing AI tools on real farming tasks, honest verdict. No affiliate deals in this series. Ever.

- "What's Changing" (`whats-changing`) — policy, tech, and market shifts translated for Irish farms
